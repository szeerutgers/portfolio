Color Theory

Understanding how colors work together in art and design

What is Color Theory?

Color theory is a framework used in art and design to understand how colors interact, combine, and affect perception. It helps creators choose color combinations that are visually appealing, harmonious, or intentionally contrasting.

Key Color Theory Terms

Color Wheel
A circular diagram that organizes colors based on relationships.
Primary Colors
Colors that cannot be created by mixing others.
Secondary Colors
Colors formed by mixing two primary colors.
Tertiary Colors
Colors made by mixing a primary and a neighboring secondary color.
Hue
The pure form of a color.
Saturation
The intensity or purity of a color.
Value (Lightness)
How light or dark a color is.
Tint
A color mixed with white.
Shade
A color mixed with black.
Tone
A color mixed with gray.
Warm Colors
Colors that evoke warmth like red and orange.
Cool Colors
Colors that evoke coolness like blue and green.
Complementary Colors
Colors opposite each other on the color wheel.
Analogous Colors
Colors next to each other on the color wheel.
Triadic Colors
Three evenly spaced colors on the wheel.
Split-Complementary
A base color plus two adjacent to its complement.
Monochromatic
Variations of a single hue.
Contrast
The difference between colors.
Color Harmony
Pleasing combinations of colors.
Color Temperature
The warmth or coolness of a color.
Chroma
The purity or intensity of a color.
Neutral Colors
Colors like black, white, and gray.
Additive Color Mixing
Mixing light (RGB) to create colors.
Subtractive Color Mixing
Mixing pigments (CMY) to create colors.
Local Color
The natural color of an object.
Simultaneous Contrast
Colors appearing different based on surroundings.
Color Context
How a color behaves depending on nearby colors.
Opacity/Transparency
How much light a color lets through.
